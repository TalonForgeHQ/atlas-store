About us

Concord empowers mid-sized companies in the healthcare, tech, professional services, and financial services sectors to make smarter operational decisions through its AI-powered Agreement Intelligence platform. Trusted by over 1,500 companies and 1 million users worldwide, Concord enables organizations to swiftly sign agreements and access crucial business data, accelerating deal closures and boosting revenue growth. As more users adopt Concord, businesses gain greater insights that enhance financial performance, streamline operations, and mitigate risks. Established in 2014, Concord has nearly a decade of leadership in agreement intelligence, turning agreements into exponential value for growing businesses.

    A nonprofit's contract process holds up fine at 20 vendor agreements. Somewhere between 50 and 100, it quietly breaks. Contracts end up scattered across a shared drive, an inbox, a filing cabinet, and one person's memory. That works until budgeting season, when someone has to open every file just to check a renewal date and rate. Concord's document management turns that pile into one searchable place — bulk upload what you already have, and AI-powered OCR pulls out vendor names, terms, and dates automatically, even from scanned or hand-signed contracts. The same shift moves contracts out of email threads too: instead of wondering who has a document or whether it's still waiting on a signature, you can see exactly what stage every agreement is in. For a team managing around 100 contracts, that's the difference between manually re-checking every agreement each year and letting automated renewal alerts flag only the ones that actually need a decision.

    How many legal entities are your construction contracts actually spread across? Most construction firms don't run as one entity. There's a holding company, a few operating subsidiaries, project-specific LLCs, maybe a joint venture or two — and each one signs its own contracts with GCs, subs, vendors, and insurers. A mid-market mechanical contractor can be running 15 to 20 legal entities. An enterprise general contractor can be running hundreds. Firms that centralize 20-plus entities into one system report that entity sales and reorganizations move significantly faster, because the contractual picture is already assembled instead of rebuilt from scratch. If leadership asked for a consolidated contract picture across every entity tomorrow, could your team pull it together — or would it take weeks?

    Sales can see contracts they shouldn't. HR agreements are one login away from anyone. Sound familiar? Operations leaders managing contracts across departments describe this same tension constantly: share too much and you create compliance risk, share too little and you become the bottleneck fielding every "can I see this" request yourself. It's one of the most common ways a spreadsheet-and-shared-drive setup breaks down once a company grows past a handful of departments. Folder-level permissions solve it without adding overhead. Contracts live in a folder structure, admins control who sees what, and deadline alerts scope automatically to each person's access level — a sales rep only gets renewal alerts for sales contracts, not the entire portfolio, and IT never touches HR agreements. Nobody maintains a manual access list, and the ops leader stops being the single point of failure for every "can I see this" question.

    The biggest excuse for staying stuck in email contracts: "We'd have to re-key hundreds of old files by hand." You wouldn't. Here's the problem underneath: teams have years of contracts scattered across inboxes, desktops, and shared drives, with no single source of truth. So a CLM migration sounds like months of manual data entry before anything gets better. The move: bulk-upload those existing contracts into the platform. OCR makes every document searchable, and AI extraction automatically pulls the parties, agreement category, lifecycle dates, renewal and termination terms, and financial values, no blank fields to fill in by hand. The result: your back catalog is structured and searchable from day one, and deadline alerts can start firing immediately because the dates are already extracted. It's the step in our 30-day email-to-CLM migration plan that makes the whole timeline realistic.
