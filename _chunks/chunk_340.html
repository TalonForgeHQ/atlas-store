AI Data Labeling Vendor Due Diligence Checklist 2026

Buyer guide · Procurement + Legal + Security + AI Governance · Published 2026-07-19

Use this checklist before purchasing AI annotation, RLHF, evaluation-data, red-team-data, programmatic labeling, or content-engineering services. It turns six vendor-specific Atlas reviews into one buyer-intent framework your procurement team can reuse.

Request a $500 / 48-hour vendor audit
Scope: This is an independent due-diligence framework, not a certification or legal opinion. Ask each vendor for current evidence and verify contract language against your own systems, data classes, regions, and risk profile.

The short answer: what evidence should a buyer request?

A mature AI data vendor should be able to connect every delivered label or preference judgment to a source asset, task, annotator or labeling function, model or workflow version, quality-review decision, tenant, region, contract or license basis, and immutable audit event. A certificate alone does not prove that chain.

The minimum useful evidence bundle is: (1) task-to-dataset provenance, (2) training-data and license provenance, (3) hostile-input and poisoning controls, (4) tenant and regional isolation evidence, and (5) retention, deletion, and cost attribution.

25-question AI data labeling vendor checklist

1. Task, asset, annotator, and model provenance

  1. Can you export a join table linking source_asset_id → task_id → annotation_id → reviewer_id → dataset_version_id → delivery_id?
  2. For programmatic labeling, can you link each output to the exact labeling-function version and conflict-resolution policy?
  3. For RLHF or evaluation data, can you identify the preference rubric, evaluator cohort, language, region, and quality-review result?
  4. Can audit events be exported in a machine-readable format without opening a support ticket?
  5. How do you preserve provenance when a dataset is filtered, merged, deduplicated, re-labeled, or used in a fine-tuning run?

2. Training-data rights and documentation

  1. What contractual basis permits collection, annotation, transformation, model training, and downstream redistribution of each data class?
  2. Can you produce dataset cards, source summaries, consent records, and rights or license metadata at delivery time?
  3. How do you identify copyrighted, personal, biometric, health, financial, confidential, or location data before work begins?
  4. Can you support a customer preparing EU AI Act technical documentation or a copyright-policy summary without reconstructing evidence manually?
  5. What is the deletion and downstream-recall process when a source record is disputed, withdrawn, or found to lack rights?

3. Prompt injection, poisoned inputs, and evaluator integrity

  1. How are embedded instructions, malicious documents, data-poisoning attempts, and tool-output injection detected before annotators or models process them?
  2. Are annotator workspaces isolated from unnecessary secrets, customer systems, and model credentials?
  3. How do you detect collusion, copied answers, rubric gaming, synthetic-account abuse, or anomalous evaluator behavior?
  4. Can you replay the exact decision path for a disputed label, safety score, or preference judgment?
  5. Which controls map to your actual threat model, and which are merely generic policy statements?

4. Tenant, workforce, and regional isolation

  1. Are customer assets, annotation queues, exports, embeddings, and quality metrics isolated by tenant?
  2. Can customers restrict processing by annotator geography, language, clearance, or employment classification?
  3. Where are raw assets, temporary files, backups, logs, and derived datasets stored and replicated?
  4. What subprocessor and cross-border transfer mechanisms cover each processing region?
  5. Are customer-managed keys, single-tenant storage, private networking, or dedicated workforces available for high-risk data?

5. Retention, deletion, incident response, and unit economics

  1. Can retention be configured separately for raw assets, annotations, reviewer notes, prompts, logs, exports, and backups?
  2. Can the vendor prove deletion across primary storage, caches, backups, work queues, and downstream derived datasets?
  3. Are critical audit records tamper-evident or WORM-capable, with tested restoration and incident timelines?
  4. Can invoices be reconciled to tasks, assets, languages, quality tiers, rework, model calls, and reviewer hours?
  5. What happens to customer evidence, exports, and deletion obligations at contract termination or vendor acquisition?

How to score the answers

ScoreEvidence qualityBuyer action
0Claim only; no artifact or ownerTreat as an open control gap
1Policy or screenshot; limited scopeRequest implementation evidence
2Export, log sample, contract clause, or tested controlValidate coverage and exceptions
3Repeatable export tied to IDs, owners, regions, and retentionAccept with monitoring

Score all 25 questions from 0–3. A raw score is less important than the distribution: any zero in provenance, data rights, tenant isolation, deletion, or incident reconstruction should trigger a documented risk acceptance or remediation condition before production use.

A 48-hour diligence sprint

  1. Hour 0–4: classify the data, jurisdictions, users, downstream models, and prohibited uses.
  2. Hour 4–12: send the 25-question request plus a sample-export request and contract-control matrix.
  3. Hour 12–24: score evidence, identify unsupported claims, and map gaps to production use cases.
  4. Hour 24–36: draft remediation conditions, compensating controls, and contract redlines.
  5. Hour 36–48: deliver the executive decision memo, evidence register, risk owners, and next review date.
