3. EU AI Act Aug 2 2026 GPAI Compliance Mapping

Article 53(d) — Public training-data summary. Cohere North is a GPAI system under the EU AI Act. The Aug 2 2026 deadline requires a publicly available summary of training data used for Command-A + Aya + any third-party-licensed fine-tuning data. The summary must be sufficiently detailed to enable downstream users to understand the model's capabilities + limitations + provenance. Most foundation-model vendors file this in week 51 of the deadline year; filing it now wins enterprise procurement by ~6 weeks.

Article 53(1)(b) — Copyright compliance policy. Cohere must implement a copyright opt-out policy for Command-A + Aya + Embed-v3 training corpora. This means a documented process where rights-holders can opt their works out of training + a verifiable machine-readable opt-out registry (e.g., robots.txt + sitemap + C2PA-style metadata) that the training pipeline respects.

Article 14 — Human oversight. Cohere Enterprise + Cohere Private Deployment must expose a documented human-oversight interface for high-risk downstream deployments (the customer builds the high-risk system ON TOP of Cohere's GPAI; Cohere owes the customer a documented way to enforce human-in-the-loop at the integration boundary).

Article 12 — Logging. Every Cohere-API call + every Cohere-Enterprise-tenant inference + every Cohere-Private-Deployment-VPC inference must produce an audit log entry with prompt/model/version + tool-call + completion + identity-assertion + tenant-isolation metadata. The log must be queryable by conversation_id across Command-A + Embed-v3 + Rerank-v3 + Aya calls in a single join (cross-stack action join-table — the same problem Cognigy 99 has).

Article 50 — Transparency. Any output from Command-A + Aya + Embed-v3 must be detectable as AI-generated when used in a customer-facing surface. Cohere's role: provide a detectability API + C2PA Content Credentials + a watermarking interface for Command-A outputs.

4. Canadian AI Act Bill C-27 + PIPEDA Compliance Mapping

Bill C-27 (Canada's proposed AI + privacy law): Cohere is headquartered in Toronto, so Bill C-27's "high-impact AI system" classification will likely apply to Command-A + Aya when deployed by Canadian federal government agencies + banks + telecoms. The current Bill C-27 draft (re-tabled 2024-2025) defines a high-impact system as one whose output materially influences decisions in employment, healthcare, finance, justice, biometrics, or critical infrastructure. Foundation models used as building blocks for these systems are caught as "general-purpose AI systems" with downstream-impact assessment obligations.

PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act): Cohere's Canadian-customer data flows through Toronto + Montreal data centers. PIPEDA's Schedule 1 (the Canadian equivalent of GDPR Art. 5 principles) requires consent + limiting use + disclosure + accuracy + safeguards + openness + individual access + challenging compliance. Command-A's training-data consent posture (opt-in vs. opt-out vs. fair-use) will be a PIPEDA Schedule-1 question when a Canadian enterprise buyer files an access-to-information request.

Canada CSA Model AI Governance Framework: The Canadian Centre for Cyber Security published a Model AI Governance Framework in 2024 that Cohere's enterprise customers will reference when assessing Command-A procurement. The framework maps to ISO 42001 + ISO 27001 + ISO 27701 + SOC 2 Type II and recommends a risk-tiered approach (Tier 1: limited impact, Tier 2: moderate, Tier 3: high). Cohere should publish a self-attestation under the CSA framework for Command-A + Aya.
