Q1 — Why is HiddenLayer in scope for EU AI Act at all?

HiddenLayer is the platform-of-record for the customer's AI-workload signals. The customer is the deployer; HiddenLayer is the platform. Aug 2 2026 deployer-readiness packs must list every platform-of-record per-customer — HiddenLayer is one of them for any AI/ML model HiddenLayer is monitoring.

Two buyer-side GRC pain points: (i) per-deployment AI-BOM has no industry standard yet, so the GRC team is building it from scratch; (ii) MITRE ATLAS evidence is required by EU AI Act Annex IV but no mapping exists to vendor signals yet — the audit deliverable solves both.

Q3 — What is the AI-BOM and why does it matter?

AI-BOM is the SBOM-equivalent for AI models. Just as SBOM has become a default SOC 2 deliverable, AI-BOM is becoming a default EU AI Act Annex IV deliverable. HiddenLayer is the canonical first-party AI-BOM issuer for production AI workloads.

Q4 — What is MITRE ATLAS evidence and how does HiddenLayer provide it?

MITRE ATLAS is the adversarial-ML threat catalogue maintained by MITRE. Each ATLAS technique maps to one or more HiddenLayer signals. The audit deliverable produces the per-signal × per-technique matrix the customer GRC team can paste into Annex IV §8 (post-market-monitoring).

The HiddenLayer platform ships quarterly. Each release changes at least one signal. Without a quarterly refresh, the AI-BOM template goes stale on the first release day, and the customer-side GRC team has to redo the work. The $497/mo retainer keeps the binder current across platform releases.
