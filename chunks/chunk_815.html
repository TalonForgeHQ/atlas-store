The five-question audit-letter wedge

  1. Event-to-meter: can any token, GPU-second, API-call, or storage event be replayed with its timestamp, idempotency key, duplicate decision, aggregation window, and rated quantity?
  2. Pricing version: which effective-dated package, volume, graduated, percentage, commitment, credit, or hybrid rule priced that event?
  3. Wallet liability: which grant, paid top-up, expiration, void, refund, or invoice application changed the credit balance?
  4. Deployment boundary: which Open Source, Lago Cloud, or Enterprise version, patch, role policy, and encryption boundary processed the data?
  5. Invoice correction: which source events, plan version, tax fields, payment reference, credit note, and reissued invoice explain the final amount?

Open source is an audit advantage and a control obligation

Inspectable source and self-hosting reduce vendor opacity. They also require version proof: commit/release, patch state, extensions, deployment configuration, and migration history must join the billable-event receipt. Otherwise two customers can run “Lago” yet produce materially different billing behavior.
