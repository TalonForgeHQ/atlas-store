Why this page exists

Humanoid robots crossed a compliance line under EU AI Act Annex III §1(a) the moment the regulation entered force on Aug 2 2026 — not §1(b) (safety component of a regulated product), but the stricter §1(a) "intended to interact with natural persons". If your humanoid robot operates in the same space as a human — warehouse aisle, retail floor, hospital corridor, hotel lobby — the auditor will assume §1(a) by default. The penalty surface is dramatically wider than for an AMR: €15M OR 3% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

The seven compliance hooks you must implement

  1. VLA (vision-language-action) version-pinning per actuation. Every motor command binds to: Helix version hash + on-device inference run ID + safety-state vector. This is your Art. 14(4) record.
  2. Functional-safety monitored stop. ISO 10218-1:2011 + ISO/TS 15066 (collaborative robots) + ISO 13849-1 PL=d on the emergency-stop chain. The robot's "stop" must be hardware, not software-only.
  3. Human-oversight operational record (Art. 14). Not "trust the robot" — you need a logged human approve/reject/edit event per non-trivial action. The Helix team calls this out as a deployment-time integration, not a model-time feature.
  4. OTA signed-firmware integrity (NIS2 Art. 21(2)(e)). Secure-boot + signed-image verification across the humanoid fleet. A single unsigned firmware push is now a notifiable incident under NIS2.
  5. §1(a) transparency labeling (Art. 50). The robot must disclose it's an AI system. Most humanoids do not currently do this in voice output ("Hello, I'm Figure 02, an AI robot") — the audit wants it logged.
  6. Post-market monitoring plan (Art. 61). Closed-loop: incident → CAPA → version-roll → customer notice → revalidation. Must include near-miss reporting (the §1(a) standard is stricter than §1(b)).
  7. Fundamental-rights impact assessment (Art. 27). Required for any §1(a) deployment in a public space. Most humanoid pilots in hotels + retail + hospitals trigger this.

The 1X NEO wedge

1X's NEO is the only consumer-targeted humanoid that ships today. Three audit hooks specific to consumer humanoids:

  • Household-privacy impact assessment (Art. 27). A humanoid living in a private home sees things GDPR never imagined. Article 27 fundamental-rights-impact is mandatory, not optional.
  • Minors-in-household safeguard. ISO/TS 15066 was written for industrial collaborative robots — there is no equivalent for child-interaction humanoids. Most auditors will fall back on ISO/TR 23482-2 anthropomorphic-robot safety.
  • Voice-output §1(a) transparency. NEO must verbally identify itself as an AI robot when first interacting with a person. Current NEO firmware does this unevenly — closed-loop revalidation is the audit response.

The Tesla Optimus wedge

Optimus is not yet generally deployed, but Tesla's gigafactory pilot already triggers §1(a) obligations. Three hooks:

  • Intra-factory fleet OTA evidence. Tesla's edge-AI competency gives Optimus the cleanest OTA story in the cohort — but the audit wants signed-image verification per unit, not just per factory.
  • In-house Tesla Foundation Model audit. Optimus runs on an in-house VLA — the EU AI Act Art. 53(1)(b) GPAI training-data transparency cascade is the audit hook that fails most often.
  • §1(a) vs §1(b) deployment-context split. Tesla is the cleanest example where the same robot crosses both §1(a) and §1(b) depending on context — factory-floor is §1(a), but a parked-and-deactivated Optimus is not a high-risk AI system at all. The intended-purpose statement in the technical documentation must call this out.

What the humanoid audit actually flags

Three patterns fail 70% of first-time humanoid audits we've mapped:

  1. "We trust the robot" safety story. Auditors will not accept model-as-safety-case. The functional-safety ISO 13849-1 PL=d chain must be hardware, not software.
  2. No rollback-and-revalidate workflow. OTA without rollback is now a NIS2 + AI Act dual violation. Helix-version-rolls need a saved pre-roll-backup.
  3. Voice-output without transparency labeling. §1(a) is unambiguous: the robot must verbally disclose it's an AI robot. Most current humanoid firmware does not do this consistently.
