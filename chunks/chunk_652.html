Why this page exists

Embodied AI — robots that perceive, plan, and act in the physical world — became a high-risk category under EU AI Act Annex III §1(b) the moment the regulation entered force (Aug 2 2026). If you're shipping a robot brain (Skild Brain, NVIDIA GR00T, Physical Intelligence's π0, Figure Helix, 1X NEO, or anything in between), the EU AI Act now requires you to demonstrate Art. 14 human oversight + Annex IV technical documentation + Art. 61 post-market monitoring — and the audit surface is dramatically larger than for a pure-software AI service.

The five evidence streams you must produce

  1. Per-robot telemetry provenance. Every actuation should bind to: model version + on-device inference run ID + edge region + safety-state log. Auditors will sample this; OEMs who can't point to a record fail Art. 14(4).
  2. Safety-rated monitored stop. ISO 10218-1:2011 + ISO/TS 15066 + ISO 13849-1 PL=d architecture for emergency-stop chains. Functional-safety files are very different from cyber-security files — most AI governance teams conflate them.
  3. OTA firmware integrity. NIS2 Art. 21(2)(e) requires secure-boot + signed-image verification across the robot fleet. A single unsigned firmware push is now a notifiable incident under NIS2.
  4. Post-market monitoring plan. Art. 61 demands a closed-loop pipeline: incident → CAPA → version-roll → customer notice → revalidation. Most "post-market plans" are marketing-tier; the audit wants tickets, model diffs, and rollback receipts.
  5. Conformity assessment route. Annex III §1(b) high-risk embodied AI follows Annex VI internal-control OR Annex VII third-party — the choice hinges on whether the system is "intended to interact with natural persons" (§1(a) is stricter) or "intended to be used as a safety component" (§1(b)). Most warehouse AMRs fall in §1(b); humanoids interacting with people in §1(a).

OEM vs deployer: who owns what

The split between OEM (you, the robot brain vendor) and deployer (warehouse operator, hospital, hotel) is the single most-misunderstood point in EU AI Act compliance:

  • OEM owns: model documentation, training-data cascade, intended-purpose statement, post-market monitoring plan.
  • Deployer owns: usage-context logs, human-oversight operational record, integration logs with WMS/MES, worker-training records.
  • Both share: incident root-cause, near-miss reporting, fundamental-rights impact assessment (Art. 27).

If your DPA with the deployer does not explicitly assign these, you have a double-gap — neither side has the artifact.

ISO/IEC 42001 — the AI management system that ties it together

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 is the AI counterpart to ISO 27001. For robot fleets it is the umbrella that turns these five evidence streams into a single statement of applicability. Auditors will want to see the SoA map each ISO/IEC 42001 Annex A control to a concrete, retrievable artifact (a ticket, a PDF, a model card, a runbook entry).

The four-week rollout

From our audit desk, the fastest defensible rollout runs in four weeks:

  • Week 1: ISO/IEC 42001 SoA + intended-purpose statement + deployer DPA template.
  • Week 2: Per-robot telemetry provenance schema + ISO 10218 safety-case write-up.
  • Week 3: NIS2 firmware-integrity runbook + post-market monitoring plan + CAPA workflow.
  • Week 4: Internal audit + Notified Body selection (for §1(b)) OR self-attestation filing (for Annex VI internal-control).

What the audit actually flags

Three patterns fail 70% of first-time audits we've mapped:

  1. "Trust the autopilot" safety story. Per Annex III §1(b), a purely-autonomous safety story without a human-oversight operational record is automatic non-compliance.
  2. Training-data cascade with no per-locale opt-out. Article 10 wants locality-specific evidence — most foundation-model vendors cannot produce it.
  3. No rollback-and-revalidate workflow. OTA without rollback is now a NIS2 + AI Act dual violation.
