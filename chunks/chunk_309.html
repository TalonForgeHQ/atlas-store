Why AI competitive intelligence needs its own audit lane

Klue's first-party homepage positions the platform as a way to collect, curate, and share competitive intelligence across the organization, with AI-curated news feeds, AI-generated battlecards, and AI-moderated buyer win-loss interviews. Eight questions to ask before approving Klue

  1. Can every card claim be traced to its source feed? Require a stable card ID, battlecard version ID, source feed ID, fetch timestamp, AI generation event ID, analyst override ID, tenant ID, and review decision trail.
  2. Is source-feed license provenance preserved? Distinguish first-party feeds the customer owns from third-party feeds the customer licenses from Klue. Record license scope, redistribution rights, attribution requirements, and revocation procedure per source.
  3. Can AI battlecard regeneration be reconstructed? Join the source feed snapshot, prompt template version, AI model identifier, completion ID, reviewer override, final card state, and distribution event in one exportable event trail.
  4. Are win-loss interviews provenance-linked? For AI-moderated buyer interviews (Klue Distill), preserve the moderator model, prompt template, transcript ID, sentiment ID, recommendation ID, and analyst review ID per interview.
  5. What prevents source-feed poisoning? Ask for evidence covering competitor-site fetch validation, prompt-injection defense, AI-card-poisoning detection, human-review gates, and rollback capabilities.
  6. Are strategist and analyst tenants isolated? Test cards, battlecards, source feeds, AI summaries, win-loss transcripts, exports, support tooling, analytics, and any AI-assist overlay layer.
  7. Does deletion propagate across cards and analytics? A source-feed retraction should reach referencing cards, AI summaries, win-loss transcripts, exports, backups, and downstream processors.
  8. Can a human review consequential AI outputs? If AI ranks competitors, summarizes news, generates battlecards, or moderates buyer interviews, record the model version, override ID, reviewer, and rollback timestamp.

Five failure modes to test

  • A battlecard cites a competitor pricing page that was retired; the card is still distributed to the enablement team without a fallback link.
  • An AI-generated battlecard summarizes an analyst-call transcript whose attribution is no longer license-cleared for redistribution.
  • A win-loss moderator summary recommends a competitor narrative that no human reviewed before distribution to product marketing.
  • A deletion request removes a strategist's source-feed access while leaving previously generated AI-summary cards referencing that feed active.
  • A source-feed update triggers 5,000 card regenerations but the prior versions are retained only in customer-side analytics exports.

Regulatory and framework hooks

  • SOC 2 CC6.1 + CC7.2: cross-tenant card and source-feed isolation; AI event-trail reconstruction.
  • GDPR Art. 5 + Art. 17 + Art. 28: deletion propagates across AI summaries + win-loss transcripts + downstream exports.
  • EU AI Act Art. 12 + Art. 14 + Art. 50: AI battlecard transparency, human oversight of moderator, Aug 2 2026 GPAI documentation.
  • ISO 42001 + NIST AI RMF MEASURE: AI evidence evaluation + governance hooks for the card-generation pipeline.
  • 12-state AI disclosure (CA SB 1001, CO SB 24-205, IL AI Video Interview Act, TX, NY, etc.): moderator and summary outputs touch strategists and buyers in regulated states.
