Why vanilla SOC 2 Type II is insufficient for AI agents in regulated workflows

SOC 2 Type II audits the controls of the customer-facing application + supporting infrastructure as it existed in the audit period. It does not audit:

  • The retrieval layer (vector DB content per tenant, similarity scores, embedding-model version pinning)
  • The prompt-injection attack surface (how a malicious bates-stamped document could subvert the demand-letter output)
  • The deletion cascade through the LLM stack (when Matter A closes, does the inference cache + embedding index + retriever index + S3 cold backups all actually purge?)
  • The per-citation provenance (which bates number is the assistant's claim sourced to, what hash, what model version)
  • The human-oversight operational record (every demand letter, the attorney's review step, the timestamp, the ack)

For a financial-services vendor (Hebbia, Glean) this is a "nice to have" until the bank CISO asks. For a medical-record AI vendor (EvenUp) this is required because HIPAA + state bar Model Rules + medical malpractice insurance carriers will ask in deposition.

Q1 — Why can't we just rely on EvenUp's SOC 2 Type II letter?

Q3 — Does HIPAA BAA cover the AI inference layer?

Only if the BAA explicitly enumerates: (a) inference cache; (b) embedding vector store; (c) retriever index; (d) audit logs; (e) sub-processors with their own BAA chain. A generic "Business Associate agrees to safeguard PHI" without those five named systems is not a BAA that survives a HIPAA OCR audit. The 12-doc binder forces the BAA to name them.

Q4 — How does ABA Model Rule 1.1 (competence) interact with AI-assisted practice?

Rule 1.1 requires "the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation." AI-assisted practice does not relax Rule 1.1. The attorney must (a) understand the AI's retrieval source; (b) verify each citation before transmission; (c) decline the AI output when the retrieval-confidence falls below threshold. The 12-doc binder gives the attorney the audit-trail evidence that Rule 1.1 was satisfied per demand package. Without it, Rule 1.1 is unsignable in a malpractice deposition.

Q5 — What does the EU AI Act August 2026 deadline actually require of EvenUp?

EvenUp's PI-firm US customers are not directly subject to the EU AI Act. However, (a) any EU-resident personal-injury claimant (tourist, expat, EU-domiciled victim) brings the matter under EU jurisdiction; (b) even non-EU vendors serving EU-data-subjects in EU-jurisdictions must comply with Art. 14 human-oversight; (c) most large US-based PI firms have EU-resident clients for cross-border tort cases. The EU AI Act Art. 14 + Art. 50 high-risk classification becomes the cleanest evidence-target because it's the most prescriptive. The 12-doc binder covers Art. 14 + Art. 50 + Annex IV automatically if EvenUp produces them.
